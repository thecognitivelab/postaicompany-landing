## 🔭 Import AI — "AI oversight is difficult; scaling laws for protein folding models; and pricing the extinction risk of AI systems"
**Issue 459, June 1.** Jack Clark delivers the most important insider account of AI-native engineering we've read this year: inside Anthropic, "many engineers no longer write code directly" — they instantiate Claude in tools and manage its output. Humans are becoming a verification layer atop a "vast virtual organization of AI agents." He also quantifies the invisible AI economy: $250B in nominal AI GDP (2025), growing ~2,600% per year in quality-adjusted terms, but largely invisible in conventional statistics because per-unit costs collapse as fast as output rises.

**Our take:** This is the Post AI thesis in one issue. The "invisible economy" problem Clark describes is exactly why we build the Post AI Index — to measure what conventional metrics miss. His RSI timeline (1–2 years) is aggressive but internally consistent with the engineering reality he describes: if 9 synthetic research agents supervised by one human already beat human baselines on scalable oversight, the slope is clear.

→ [Read on Jack Clark's site](https://jack-clark.net/2026/06/01/import-ai-459-ai-oversight-is-difficult-scaling-laws-for-protein-folding-models-and-pricing-the-extinction-risk-of-ai-systems/)

## 📊 Interconnects — "Open and closed models are on different exponentials" + "Farewell Ai2"
**June 1–2.** Nathan Lambert leaves Ai2 after leading the Olmo open model program, launching something new (stealth). His companion piece argues open and closed models are on diverging curves: where marginal intelligence gains drive value, closed wins; where cost, customizability, and ecosystem compound, open has structural advantages. The open model bonanza continues — Gemma 4, DeepSeek V4, Kimi K2.6, MiMo 2.5, GLM-5.1 all shipping in weeks.

**Our take:** Lambert's framework is more useful than "open will catch up / won't." The split-exponential thesis explains why both sides keep winning simultaneously: Anthropic charges ahead on frontier reasoning while Harvey (fine-tuned Kimi 2.6) beats Opus 4.7 on legal benchmarks at 11× lower cost. The question isn't which side wins — it's how fast the gap moves, and for which tasks.

→ [Open and closed models are on different exponentials](https://www.interconnects.ai/p/open-and-closed-models-are-on-different)
→ [Farewell Ai2](https://www.interconnects.ai/p/farewell-ai2)

## 📰 The Batch — "Qwen3.7-Max Challenges Google for Third Place, AI Saves Whales, Fine-Tuning Breaks Copyright Alignment"
**June 5.** Andrew Ng's weekly: Qwen3.7-Max moves past Gemini in key benchmarks, fine-tuning erases safety guardrails as a side effect, and conservation AI applications show real-world impact (whale detection from satellite imagery). Ng frames regulatory capture as the real AI policy danger.

**Our take:** The Qwen story matters because it's not just "China catching up" — it's "open weights catching up." Qwen3.7-Max is accessible, fine-tunable, and now competitive at the frontier. The fine-tuning/alignment breakage finding is the sleeper: as domain-specific models proliferate (Harvey for law, Ramp Stack for accounting), safety is increasingly a per-instance problem, not a frontier-lab problem.
