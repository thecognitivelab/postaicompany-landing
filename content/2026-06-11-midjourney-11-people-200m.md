# 11 People, $200M. Then 107 People, $500M. What Midjourney Knows That You Don't

**Post AI Weekly — Edition 001 · June 11, 2026**

---

## THE SIGNAL

In 2023, Midjourney hit $200 million in annual recurring revenue. The company had no sales team. No marketing department. No free tier. No venture capital. And depending on which month you count, somewhere between 11 and 40 employees. By 2025, revenue reached $500 million with approximately 107 people — roughly $4.7 million per employee. The average publicly traded SaaS company generates about $300,000 per employee. Google does $1.8 million. Meta, $2.2 million. Midjourney doesn't just beat these numbers — it operates in an entirely different gravitational field.

The question isn't "how did an AI company get efficient." The question is: what did David Holz decide *not* to build? No sales org. No marketing team. No free tier to "capture market share." No HR department scaling in lockstep with revenue. Every function that a traditional company treats as non-negotiable infrastructure, Midjourney treated as optional — and then proved it was.

This edition is about those decisions. Because the difference between a company doing $300K per employee and one doing $4.7M isn't AI. It's architecture.

---

## THE NUMBERS

**$18M — Revenue per employee at Midjourney's peak efficiency (2023).** With an estimated 11 employees and $200M ARR, the ratio was unprecedented. By 2025, with 107 employees and $500M ARR, it settled to roughly $4.7M per employee — still 15x the SaaS average. (Sacra, Forbes Mar 2026, GetLatka)

**$0 — Total venture capital raised since founding.** Holz rejected outside investment from day one. When Meta partnered with Midjourney in August 2025 to license its "aesthetic technology," the $50M involved was non-dilutive — IP revenue, not equity. Compare: Stability AI raised over $100M in VC, burned through it with open-source models and aggressive hiring, reported quarterly revenue under $5M, and saw its CEO depart amid layoffs. (Sacra, Contrary Research, ProductGrowth)

**$0 — Marketing spend. Ever.** Midjourney launched as a Discord bot in a public channel. Every image generation was visible to every other user. The `/imagine` command produced 4 variations in ~30 seconds, displayed progressively via Discord's message-editing API. Each result was simultaneously a product demo, an inspiration feed, and an ad — with zero media budget. The Discord server hit 1 million users in 3 months and now exceeds 21 million members. (Contrary Research, ProductGrowth)

**40% — Block's workforce reduction in February 2026.** Jack Dorsey cut 4,000 jobs from Block (Square, Cash App, Afterpay), citing "intelligence tools" as the driver. Stock surged 24% on the announcement. The same month, Meta began laying off 8,000 employees — 10% of its workforce — with Zuckerberg explicitly linking the cuts to AI-driven efficiency. According to Layoffs.fyi, nearly 80,000 tech workers have been impacted by AI-cited layoffs in 2026 so far. The trends are converging: AI-native companies build lean from the start, and legacy companies are now amputating to catch up. (WSJ, CNN, CNBC, Layoffs.fyi)

## THE SHIFT

Let's be precise about what "AI-native" actually means here. It's not about using AI tools. Every company uses AI tools now. The difference is that Midjourney treated organizational structure itself as a design surface — and made every decision in service of keeping headcount decoupled from revenue.

**Distribution before product.** Holz didn't build a website and then try to attract users. He embedded Midjourney inside Discord, a platform that already had 175 million active users when Midjourney launched in 2022. The `/imagine` command didn't require anyone to visit a landing page, create an account, or learn a new interface. Users were already there. The community was already there. The social dynamics — watching strangers generate images in real time, wanting to try it yourself — were native to the platform, not engineered by a growth team. Contrast this with the standard SaaS playbook: build product → build marketing site → run ads → hire SDRs → build sales team. Midjourney skipped steps two through five.

**Pay-to-play, from day one.** Midjourney offered a 25-image trial, then removed free access entirely in March 2023. Every user pays. Pricing runs from $10/month to $120/month. This is heresy in SaaS orthodoxy, where free tiers and freemium funnels are treated as non-negotiable. But when your product delivers value that takes 30 seconds to demonstrate — type words, get images — you don't need to give it away. Midjourney reached profitability six months after public launch. Stability AI, which open-sourced its models and offered free access, burned cash and struggled to convert users.

**No managers.** Midjourney's founding team was Holz plus 8 engineers, 1 legal, and 1 finance person. The org chart hasn't grown management layers as revenue scaled from $200M to $500M. This isn't unique to Midjourney — it's a pattern. The ByteIota analysis of tiny teams found that AI companies operate best with generalists, structures without hierarchy, and what they call a "player-coach model" where leaders execute roughly half the time and guide the rest. The coordination burden that traditional companies solve by adding managers? In an AI-native company, tools and agents absorb much of it. The bottleneck shifts from "can we build this?" to "do we trust each other's judgment?"

**The counterexample is everywhere.** Block's 40% layoff, Meta's 8,000 cuts, Intuit's 17% reduction — these aren't AI companies getting lean. These are legacy companies discovering they're overbuilt. They hired for a model where revenue and headcount grew in lockstep, and now they're discovering that AI breaks that equation. The tragedy is that they're cutting from a position of reaction rather than design. Midjourney never had the fat to cut because Holz never added it.

**The question this raises for every founder and executive:** If you were starting your company today, from scratch, which roles would you actually hire for? Not which roles your org chart says you need — which roles create value that AI genuinely can't replicate? The answer is almost certainly a smaller number than your current headcount. Every role that exists because "that's how companies work" is a role Midjourney proved you can live without.
