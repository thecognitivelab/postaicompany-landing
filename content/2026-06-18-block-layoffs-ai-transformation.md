# The AI-First Layoff: Block, Meta, and Intuit Didn't Get Lean — They Discovered They Were Overbuilt

**Post AI Weekly — Edition 002 · June 18, 2026**

---

## THE SIGNAL

In February 2026, Jack Dorsey cut Block's workforce from 10,000 to just under 6,000 — a reduction of roughly 40%. His shareholder letter didn't cite market conditions, overhiring, or the need to "rightsize." He cited "intelligence tools." The stock surged 24% in after-hours trading. Three months later, Meta began cutting 8,000 jobs (10% of its workforce) while scrapping 6,000 open roles. That same week, Intuit eliminated 3,000 positions (17%) and simultaneously raised its full-year revenue guidance by $2.5 billion. None of these companies were in crisis. All were profitable. All were growing. The market rewarded each one for getting smaller.

The lesson isn't that AI is eliminating jobs. It's that these companies discovered a mathematical truth they'd been ignoring for a decade: revenue doesn't require proportional headcount anymore. They weren't "getting lean." They were admitting they'd been overbuilt all along.

---

## THE NUMBERS

**Block: 4,000+ jobs eliminated (40% of workforce, Feb 2026).** Dorsey's shareholder letter stated: "Intelligence tools have changed what it means to build and run a company." The reduction took Block from ~10,200 employees to under 6,000 — the single largest percentage cut by a major U.S. tech company citing AI as the primary driver. Block's Q4 2025 gross profit was $2.87 billion, up 24% year-over-year. The restructuring charge: $450–500 million. Full-year 2026 EPS guidance raised to $3.66, well above analyst consensus of $3.22. Stock surged 24% in after-hours trading. (WSJ, CNBC, Block Shareholder Letter, Feb 2026)

**Meta: 8,000 jobs cut (10%), plus 6,000 open roles eliminated (May 2026).** Zuckerberg's April memo framed the cuts as "part of our continued effort to run the company more efficiently and to allow us to offset the other investments we're making" — primarily the $145 billion capex plan for AI infrastructure. CFO Susan Li told analysts: "We don't really know what the optimal size of the company will be in the future." Unlike Block, Meta's stock underperformed — down ~7% YTD — suggesting the market distinguishes between structural redesign and cost-shifting. (CNBC, May 2026)

**Intuit: ~3,000 jobs cut (17%), May 2026.** Same day as the layoff announcement, Intuit raised FY2026 revenue guidance to $21.35 billion (up from $18.8B in FY2025) and disclosed multi-year AI partnerships with Anthropic and OpenAI. Revenue has grown 68% since FY2022 while headcount is declining. CEO Sasan Goodarzi's internal memo cited "too many management layers" and the need for "greater velocity." The previous round — 1,800 jobs in July 2024 — was also explicitly tied to AI investment. (Reuters, HCAMag, May 2026)

**~128,000 tech workers laid off YTD 2026, across 140+ companies.** According to Layoffs.fyi, the daily average of 882 cuts is outpacing 2025's 674/day. The 48-hour window of May 19–20 alone saw 16,000+ confirmed cuts from Meta, Intuit, Cisco, ZoomInfo, and Cloudflare. Nearly 50% of affected positions were attributed to AI-driven restructuring. At the World Economic Forum in January 2026, two executives told Reuters that companies already planning layoffs would use AI as justification. (Layoffs.fyi, Reuters, KRON4, May 2026)

## THE SHIFT

The easy narrative is "AI is replacing workers." That's true in the narrowest sense, but it misses what's actually happening inside these organizations. Block, Meta, and Intuit aren't experiencing the same phenomenon. They're converging on the same discovery from different starting points — and the distinction matters enormously for anyone running a company today.

**These weren't crisis cuts. They were architecture corrections.** Block's gross profit was growing at 24% YoY. Intuit raised guidance while announcing layoffs — a move that would be incoherent if the company were retrenching. Meta is spending $145 billion on AI infrastructure while cutting 14,000 total positions (layoffs plus scrapped open roles). These companies aren't shrinking because they're failing. They're shrinking because the math connecting headcount to output has permanently changed. When a company cuts 17% of its workforce on the same day it raises revenue guidance by $2.5 billion, it isn't managing a downturn. It's acknowledging that its previous staffing model was built for a different equation.

**The market is sorting companies by their structural efficiency, not their growth rate.** Block stock jumped 24% on layoff news. Intuit's stock fell 5%. Meta is underperforming megacap peers. The market isn't blindly rewarding job cuts — it's evaluating whether the cut signals genuine structural change or desperate cost-shifting. Block's cut was decisive, one-time, and transparent about the reason. Meta's was fragmented across multiple rounds, accompanied by internal dysfunction (the MCI tracking tool, the employee petition, the 39% decline in culture rating on Blind). The difference in market reaction tells you everything about what investors are actually pricing: not headcount reduction, but evidence that leadership understands the new math.

**The companies getting this right never built the fat in the first place.** Last week's edition documented Midjourney: 11 people, $200M ARR. Now 107 people, $500M. Every role was interrogated: "does this create value AI can't replicate?" Cursor (Anysphere) reached $100M ARR with roughly 20 people. These AI-native companies didn't "get lean." They were architected from day one around a principle that legacy companies are now learning through amputation: headcount is a design choice, not a default.

**The Klarna cautionary tale illustrates the gap between cutting and designing.** In 2024, Klarna's CEO announced that AI had replaced 700 customer service agents. By early 2026, the company was quietly rebuilding human support teams, reassigning engineers and marketers to phone duty. The AI chatbot could handle 2.3 million conversations — but couldn't handle the ones that mattered. Klarna cut first and designed second. The companies building AI-native architectures are doing the reverse.

**The question every executive should be asking isn't "how many people can AI replace?"** It's "what was our headcount model built for, and is that model still valid?" Block, Meta, and Intuit all hired for a world where revenue growth required proportional headcount growth. That world ended. The difference between reacting to that change and anticipating it — between amputation and architecture — is the difference between a stock that surges 24% and one that drifts sideways while morale collapses.
