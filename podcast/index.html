Midjourney: 11 People, $200M

The Debate — 3 Rounds

Round 1: Outlier or Pattern?

Marco argues Midjourney was in the right place at the right time — Discord + generative images. Not replicable. Lena counters with Cursor (20 people, $100M ARR) and Lovable (146 people, $400M ARR, no sales team). The numbers suggest a pattern, not an anomaly.

Round 2: The Post AI Index

Lena brings the data: AI-native startups average $3.48M per employee vs $610K for traditional SaaS. Marco asks: "How many companies beyond these six examples?" Lena pushes back: "The top 10 AI-native startups average 24 employees and $3.48M. The top 10 traditional SaaS average 21,000 employees."

Round 3: Does This Scale?

Marco: "Midjourney went from 11 to 107 people. The model breaks as you grow." Lena: "107 people at $500M is $4.7M per employee — still 15x the SaaS average. Is that 'breaking' or 'scaling efficiently'?" They disagree on whether the ratios hold at enterprise scale. They end the round still disagreeing.

Excerpt

Marco: I don't buy that Midjourney is a blueprint. It's an outlier. It was the right product, at the right moment, on the right platform. That's not strategy. That's being in the right place at the right time.
Lena: Here's what the data actually says. Cursor: 20 people, $100M ARR. Lovable: 146 people, $400M ARR. ElevenLabs: $500M ARR. Perplexity: 5x revenue growth on 1.34x headcount. This isn't one outlier. It's a distribution.
Marco: Okay, but those are all software companies. Show me a manufacturing company doing $4M per employee. Show me a logistics company.
Lena: That's exactly the point. The question isn't whether every industry hits Midjourney's ratio. It's whether every industry moves in that direction. And the data says yes.
